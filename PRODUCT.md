# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Joe Rogan Experience listeners, Bitcoin/crypto community members, and long-term Michael Saylor followers who land on the page from X or a dLogos link. They are mid-scroll on social media, deciding in seconds whether this looks legitimate enough to sign and share. The job to be done: read the petition, trust it enough to add their name, and re-share it to apply social pressure.

## Product Purpose

A single-page open letter / petition campaign asking Joe Rogan to have Michael Saylor on The Joe Rogan Experience. It exists to convert casual readers into signers and re-sharers, and to look credible enough that JRE's booking team takes the documented demand seriously. Success is both signature/share volume and the page reading as a legitimate, official-feeling record rather than a fan-made meme page.

## Brand Personality

Official, deadpan, serious — a government case-file tone. The page presents itself like a filed petition document: stamps, case numbers, routing slips, formal letter copy. The "we're not asking nicely anymore" line is the one moment of edge inside an otherwise bureaucratic, deadpan register. Voice is plain and declarative, never jokey or meme-coded.

## Anti-references

- Generic crypto/SaaS landing pages: gradient text, glassmorphism cards, hero-metric-with-gradient-accent templates.
- Warm cream/parchment/sand "AI default" aesthetic — explicitly moved away from the original parchment-scroll letter treatment toward a flatter, sharper, document-style dark UI.
- Meme-page energy or irony — the page should read as a real filed record, not a joke.

## Design Principles

- The page is a document, not a marketing hero — structure it like a case file (banner, case info, exhibit, supporting statement), not like a SaaS landing page.
- Deadpan over decorative — flat panels, hairline borders, monospace case-file labels; no glow, soft shadows, or pill shapes standing in for "official."
- One accent, used deliberately — orange carries CTAs and key data points against black/white/dark-grey; it should never compete with itself.
- Mechanics are sacred — the sign/share/download flow (counter, button, modal, X compose) must keep working through every visual iteration; never break IDs or JS hooks while restyling.
- Legitimacy is the persuasion device — every visual choice should make the page feel more like an official record JRE's team would take seriously, not less.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Standard WCAG AA: body text ≥4.5:1 contrast, large/bold text ≥3:1, visible focus states on interactive elements, and a `prefers-reduced-motion` fallback for all animation (already implemented for the sign-button pulse and other transitions).
